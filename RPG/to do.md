Letting a level 1 beat a high-level monster
The reason this feels hard specifically for a browser/idle game is that idle games usually get their "skill" entirely from theorycrafting (build knowledge), not execution — there's no room for "get good at dodging" when combat is just numbers ticking. But you don't need reflex-based skill to make execution matter. You need decision-based skill, which works fine over HTTP with zero latency sensitivity and is genuinely mobile-friendly.
Here's the mechanic I'd build the whole combat loop around: every round, the enemy telegraphs its next move before it happens, and the round becomes a puzzle of picking the right response from your 6 runes. Not "click attack" — "the ogre is winding up a Heavy Strike, do you Block, Dodge, or just race it down first."That gives you two independent axes of skill, and either one alone can let a level 1 outperform a level 50:
Build skill (pre-fight). If enemies expose a trait or two before you commit — armored, volatile, regenerating — and some of your runes are mediocre in general but hard-counter a specific trait, a player who reads the room and brings a precisely-teched loadout beats a bigger but generically-built character. It's also a good reason to keep weak-looking runes in the economy instead of power-creeping them out: they're not weak, they're situational, and situational means tradeable.
Execution skill (in-fight). This is the loop above. A player who correctly reads and answers eight telegraphs in a row simply isn't taking damage from anything they should've blocked — a real, learnable, non-random gap between a good and bad player, independent of gear.
I'd add one more layer: a break window. If you counter a telegraph perfectly — not just adequately, the specific rune that hard-counters that specific move, not any old block — the enemy doesn't just whiff, it's exposed for a round: bonus damage taken. That turns survival into punishment. A well-played level 1 doesn't out-tank a level 50, they solve the fight in six rounds instead of surviving sixty.
One principle I'd protect on purpose, because it resolves real tension between two of your own pillars: let luck decide what you're facing, but let skill decide whether you win. If dice also swing individual rounds, "skill beats level" quietly turns into "sometimes RNG lets you win," which is a much weaker promise. Keep the surprise — rare monster, rare loot, dungeon composition — upstream of the fight, and keep the fight itself readable and fair.
For idle play, which the genre demands, give the loop an auto-battle mode that picks the statistically safest response each round — good enough for content at or below your level, never good enough to win a fight above it. That cap is what actually protects the promise: if auto-battle could win those fights too, skill wouldn't be the thing doing the winning. Melvor already has a taste of this, incidentally — special attacks gated behind a resource you choose when to spend — so you're extending a mechanic idle players already half know, not inventing something foreign to the genre.
A few concrete telegraphs to make this less abstract:

Charging slash — telegraphed one round ahead, heavy damage. Parry it exactly and the enemy is staggered (skips its next turn) instead of just no-selling the hit.
Venom spit — telegraphs incoming poison stacks. A generic block reduces it, a dedicated antivenom rune negates it, and a "convert poison to shield" rune — a nice pairing with your negative-value runes — turns the whole exchange into a buff.
Enrage cycle (higher-level monsters) — every fourth move is a huge telegraphed ultimate, but the enemy is exposed the round before it if you forced it to waste earlier turns defending. That's bait-and-punish: you're shaping the fight, not just reacting to it.

How weapons, skills, and runes should relate
Worth nailing down before you build more on the existing rarity system: are skills baked into weapons, or are runes your skills? I'd keep these as two separate layers so uniqueness and strategy don't fight each other.
Weapon = your kit. Each unique weapon roll grants one to three innate abilities — this is where "no two weapons are the same" actually lives, and it's what makes a rare drop exciting: you got new toys, not just bigger numbers.
Runes = how you play that kit. The universal six-slot, value-ten loadout, independent of which weapon you're holding, that tunes, supports, or synergizes with whatever your weapon gave you — or stands alone, since your burn-plus-haste combo doesn't need to care what you're wielding.
This also answers a question your market will eventually ask: unique weapons are inherently hard to price, since there's nothing to compare a listing against, but runes are comparatively standardized — same rune, same value, wherever it drops. I'd lean on runes as your primary liquid trade good, and treat unique weapons more like found treasure that occasionally gets auctioned. More on that below.

Tightening up the rune system
The core budget — six slots, value capped at ten, negative values buy room but cost you something — is solid. A few additions:
Give every drawback a named payoff, on purpose. Your burn-into-haste example is the actual pattern to generalize: a negative-value rune should always have at least one positive-value rune elsewhere in the pool that specifically rewards the exact drawback it creates, not just "is generally good." A couple more in that spirit, to show the range:

Blood price (lose health each round, negative value) pairs with crimson momentum (below half health, gain an extra rune activation that round) — a glass-cannon build rewarded for staying dangerously low.
Reckless swing (your attacks also clip you) pairs with vengeful return (any damage you take this round, including self-inflicted, adds bonus damage to your next hit) — you're rewarded for hitting yourself harder.

Making sure the drawback side of the ledger is never a trap and always a door is honestly more of the real design work here than any individual rune.
Give runes an element and a method, not just a value. Something like fire, ice, poison, physical, arcane crossed with offense, defense, utility, drawback. This does three jobs at once: lets weak-looking runes be secretly strong against specific enemy traits, gives your generation system clean buckets to roll from, and gives your UI an easy way to filter what could otherwise become an overwhelming list once you've got hundreds of these.
Watch for budget exploits. If ten small minus-one runes buy unlimited plus-one headroom, someone will find the degenerate stack eventually. A soft cap on how much negative value you can bank, or diminishing returns past a couple of drawback runes, keeps "penalty as currency" from becoming a solved min-max loop.
A rough shape, if it helps:
Rune {
  id, name, value: -3..+5,
  element: fire | ice | poison | physical | arcane,
  method: offense | defense | utility | drawback,
  effect: { trigger, magnitude, condition },
  counters: [enemy_trait, ...]   // optional hard-counter tags
}
Making "no two are the same" sustainable
Hand-authoring unique stats for every weapon and every enemy doesn't scale once you've got real content volume. You'll want procedural variety on top of a much smaller set of hand-built pieces.
Base archetype plus rolled affixes — the Diablo/PoE shape: a "warhammer" base, then a pool of prefix and suffix modifiers rolled at generation time, gated by rarity. This is probably close to what your extended rarity system already does, worth confirming since it's the right foundation. Reserve true hand-authored uniques — named, fixed kit, capital U — as rare, build-defining moments, distinct from procedurally-rolled rares. That split gives you both infinite variety and curated holy-grail drops.
Enemies get the same treatment, and it solves your surprise pillar for free. A low-level dungeon rolling a "high-level monster" doesn't need its own high-level template — it's a base low-level monster with two or three modifiers stacked on (colossal, runic, volatile), the same way champion or rare-monster affixes work in most ARPGs. Same base creature, wildly different threat, and the surprise generates itself instead of needing to be hand-placed.
One UX cost to plan for now: if nothing is directly comparable, "is this upgrade better?" becomes real cognitive load once players have seen a few hundred items. A simple stat-delta comparison — this versus currently equipped, with up or down indicators — goes a long way toward keeping "everything is unique" feel exciting instead of exhausting.
Where luck and generative AI actually plug in
Worth being deliberate about this split early, especially once trading exists: keep anything that touches balance — item rolls, enemy modifiers, rune values — as deterministic, seeded, server-side generation. Weighted tables, not live model output. The moment a tradeable economy exists, any exploitable number generator becomes a duplication vector, and "the model produced a rune with the wrong value" is a far worse bug in a game with player trading than in a single-player one.
Where a live generative layer earns its cost is flavor, not numbers: naming a freshly-rolled unique item, writing a one-line dungeon omen, giving a rare encounter some unexpected lore. Cheap to get slightly wrong, never expensive to get wrong. If cost or latency matters, batch-generate a pool of flavor text offline rather than calling out live per encounter.
For the surprise itself, I'd add a lightweight omen layer: a visible but vague pre-fight hint — something's off here — that tells the player without fully spoiling it, and lets them choose to engage or bail. Surprise that respects the player's ability to opt out reads as exciting. Surprise that ambushes with zero warning reads as unfair, especially once death has a real cost.
Trading and the market
Two things worth flagging before you build much of this. Because most items are unique, they're inherently hard to price — nothing to compare a listing against. That's a good reason to lean on runes as the primary tradeable good and treat unique weapons more like a collectible market than a liquid one. And once real trading exists, the server needs to be the source of truth for what a player owns and what it rolled — any client-side-only combat resolution or loot generation becomes a cheating vector that poisons the shared economy, not just one save file.
I'd also build this last of your major systems, not first. It's the one most likely to need reworking once you see how players actually value things, and it depends on itemization already being stable.
